Weigelt, P., Daniel Kissling, W., Kisel, Y., Fritz, S.A., Karger, D.N., Kessler, M., Lehtonen, S., Svenning, J.-C., Kreft, H., 2015. Global patterns and drivers of phylogenetic structure in island floras. Scientific Reports 5, 12213. https://doi.org/10.1038/srep12213

Summary: Authors test general mechanisms that lead to the formation of plant assemblages on a global scale taking islands as its main focal point
Evaluation: It's a useful paper that would be good to keep in mind as tries to explain in a very broad scale the mechanisms that originate plant assemblages. It's worth to test if considering lower scale case would bring more useful information to the table

Hypotheses: (group differences): global patterns and determinants of phylogenetic structure vary among taxonomic groups due to differences in their dispersal ability, climatic adaptations, distribution patterns and diversification rates; ●(dispersal filtering): phylogenetic diversity and overdispersion increase with factors that facilitate dispersal to islands (e.g. low island isolation); ●(environmental filtering) phylogenetic diversity and overdispersion are higher if island environmental conditions fit bioclimatic requirements of a clade (e.g. tropical climate for palms); ●(in-situ speciation): factors that increase the probability of speciation on islands (e.g. larger islands area) are negatively related to phylogenetic overdispersion
Objectives: Link phylogenetic assemblage structure to island characteristics worldwide; ●test dispersal filtering vs environmental filtering; ●shed light on th origin of insular plant diversity; ●investigating the effects of abiotic island characteristics related to dispersal, environmental filtering and speciation on the phylogenetic structure
Justifications: Quantifying phylogenteic assemblage structure allows inferences about species assemblages processes; ●links between patterns of plant diversity and environmental variables are well documented but not much understanding of the underlying process; ●phylogenetic information can be used to infer assembly processes
Expectations: If dispersal-related traits show a strong phylogenetic signal(i.e. closely related species share similar dispersal abilities, then dispersal filtering should lead to phylogenetically clustered island assemblages
Remarks: A large amount of variation in phylogenetic structure of island floras can be explained by physical and bioclimatic island characteristics; ●drivers of phylogenetic structure have strong group-specific components, which supports the group differences hypothesis; ●environmental filtering plays a strong part on the phylogenetic structure of island floras; ●phylogenetically conserved physiologcal constraints of each group determine which factors act as environmental filters
Methods: phylogenetic diversity using Faith's PD (sum of all branch lengths representing the species of an island in a clade's pylogeny; ●degree of phylogenetic clustering vs. overdispersion ('i.e. higher vs. lower relatedness than expected by chance, measured as standardized effect size of mean pairwise phylogenetic distances (MPD); ●comparison between major plant clades (angiosperms, ferns, palms); ●dated family-level phylogeny for angiosperms and on dated genus-level phylogenies for palms and ferns; ●null models randomly shuffling all included species at the tips of the trees

Carvajal‐Endara, S., Hendry, A.P., Emery, N.C., Davies, T.J., 2017. Habitat filtering not dispersal limitation shapes oceanic island floras: species assembly of the Galápagos archipelago. Ecology Letters 20, 495–504. https://doi.org/10.1111/ele.12753

Objectives: Evaluates association of phylogenetic structure with its potential drivers
Expectations: If dispersal were the primary process shaping island phylogenetic structure, it is expected that more isolated islands to show greater phylogenetic clustering (assuming phylogenetic conservatism of dispersal traits).; ●if dispersal was more important, the presence of species on the archipelago should be best explained by their ability to disperse across long distances
Remarks: Ability to detect climate signal despite limitations of climatic data available suggests that environmental filtering is strong; ●Habitat filtering rather than dispersal limitation was likely the predominant process structuring plant species composition; ●Larger islands show phylogenetic overdispersion as a result of environmenal filtering, as larger islands harbours multiple habitats

Kissling, W.D., Eiserhardt, W.L., Baker, W.J., Borchsenius, F., Couvreur, T.L.P., Balslev, H., Svenning, J.-C., 2012. Cenozoic imprints on the phylogenetic structure of palm species assemblages worldwide. PNAS 201120467. https://doi.org/10.1073/pnas.1120467109

Hypotheses: As a consequence of in situ diversification regional species assemblages are expected to consist predominantly of species that are relatively closely related due to dispersal barriers; ●High net diversification should lead to phylogenetically clustered assemblages at regional scales
Objectives: Explore how Cenozoic events influenced palm assemblages as measured through phylogenetic structure
Methods: dated genus level phylogenetic tree of palms; ●focuses on a specific taxonomic group at a global scale
